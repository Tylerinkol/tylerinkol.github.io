Are the 2017 Golden State Warriors the best team in the past 20 years?

Tyler Rinkol / 2021-04-03

NBA fans have argued for years, who is the best NBA team ever. Which is a great argument to have, but looking at today’s basketball, years 2000 to 2021, who has the best team during that period?

When you think of todays basketball, you think of good shooting and stars taking over the game. Looking over the years, there has not been a team that has a set of shooters on their team until… the Golden State Warriors. When you think of the greatest team in the past 20 years, you probably think of the 2015-16 Golden State Warriors. I wouldn’t blame you, but I am here to tell you that you are wrong. Yes, they did win 73 games but when talking about the greatest team, you have to have a championship to your name. That’s why I chose the 2016-17 Golden State Warriors. If you don’t believe so, let’s go take a look at some numbers.

The first thing that I wanted to check out was the offensive Z score for every team. You may be wondering what a Z score is. A Z score is a statistical measure of the distance or a mean. It’s a good thing because a Z-score puts context on how good a team was during their era. So in order to find how good the Warriors were on offense. I had to find the mean of Points, Assists, and Rebounds for each team.

warriorzscore <- warriors %>% 
  group_by(Season, Team) %>% 
  summarise(
    PtsAvg = mean(PTS), 
    ReboundAvg = mean(TRB), 
    AssistAvg = mean(AST))
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'Season'. You can override using the `.groups` argument.

In order to find out the Z score, we need to have it as numeric function, so we are able to find the mean of it. I did that for points,rebounds, and assists. Then I was able to find out the TotalZscore by adding those three up.

teamzscore <- warriorzscore %>% 
  mutate(
    PtsZscore = as.numeric(scale(PtsAvg, center = TRUE, scale = TRUE)),
    RebZscore = as.numeric(scale(ReboundAvg, center = TRUE, scale = TRUE)),
    AstZscore = as.numeric(scale(AssistAvg, center = TRUE, scale = TRUE)),
    TotalZscore = PtsZscore + RebZscore + AstZscore)

When looking at the Z-scores for all 20 years, the 2017 Warriors were second. Right under the 2016 Warriors. Now the 2016 Warriors did beat the 2017 Warriors with points Z-score and rebounding Z-score. The 2017 Warriors did win the assist Z-score battle. The 2017 Warriors were not a rebounding team. Mostly because they made most of their shots so they didn’t have to rebound. But a key thing to look out for is that Kevin Durant missed up to 19 games during the 2017 season. I believe if he would’ve played those 19 games, their points Z-score would’ve gone up. I am not saying by a lot, but just enough to cut down the difference just a little bit more. But looking at the rest of the teams, they were all really good playoff teams. So judging that they were second, it was a good sight to see.

The next thing that I wanted to look at was how well the Warriors shot the beyond the arc and inside of it too. Lots of analyst call Curry and Thompson, the “Splash brothers” and they have definitely earned that nickname. The way I did this was going to look at teams 2 point percentage average and 3 point percent average. I had to make sure to summarize the totals for both percentages. Then I made sure to filter the best shooting teams to have a better representation of where the Warriors are compared to the better teams of the past 20 years.

warriorsscatter <- warriors %>% 
  group_by(Season, Team) %>%
  summarise(
    Twopointpct = sum(X2P.),
    Threepointpct = sum(X3P.)) %>%
  filter(Twopointpct > 0.46 & Threepointpct < 0.43)
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'Season'. You can override using the `.groups` argument.

The thing with this comparison, is to find the mean of both 2 point and 3 point percentages. I was able to do that here.

warriorsscatter %>%

Finally, I wanted to look at how bad the warriors beat up on teams based on their offensive rating. I knew already that the Warriors just beat up on teams based on their offense, but I wanted to see how it would compare to other teams. So I summarized the margin of victory a team had and also their offensive rating based on the miscellaneous stats I pulled in from above.

warriorsmov <- NBAmiss %>%
  group_by(Team,Season) %>%
  summarise(
    Marginvictory = sum(MOV),
    Offensiverating = sum(ORtg))
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'Team'. You can override using the `.groups` argument.

But when looking at the data, I wanted to make sure that it this comparison is even relevant. So we need to first look at the p-value. The p-value is set at 2.2e-16, which means that it is, 2.2 times 10 to the -16 power which is what we wanted. So, we know that this comparison is not random at all.Then we need to look at the adjusted R-squared which is set at 0.426 which we can predict that 42 percent of the offensive rating is compared to margin of victory.

fit <- lm(Marginvictory ~ Offensiverating, data = warriorsmov)
